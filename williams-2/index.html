04 · The Process
04 The selling process

From listing appointment to handing over the keys.

Most listings move through this in 30 to 60 days, depending on price point and market conditions.

STEP I

Listing appointment

I walk through your home, ask a lot of questions, and listen. We talk about your timeline, your goals, and what condition the house is actually in. I do not bring a price to this meeting. I bring questions.

STEP II

Pricing and strategy

Within 48 hours after our appointment, I prepare a real CMA based on what I saw at your home, recent sold comps, active competition, and current buyer behavior. I bring you a recommended price, the rationale, and a marketing plan specific to your property.

STEP III

Pre-launch prep

Photographer, drone, video, staging if we agreed on it, signage, MLS prep. I write the listing description and you approve it before it goes live.

If you want to avoid surprises during the buyer's inspection, this is the time to consider doing your own pest and home inspection in advance. We can either fix the issues we find before listing, or factor them into the price. Either way, we remove the biggest unknown from the deal.

STEP IV

Pre-market outreach and launch

Coming Soon status doesn't exist in our region yet, so here is how I build pre-launch interest instead. I distribute the listing to the broker network in our area to surface buyers who are already looking. I review my own database for matches.

If I find a buyer through my own database, Kayla represents them. I do not double-end. A lot of agents will reduce fees or take both sides, but I do not believe I can accurately represent both parties' interests in the same transaction.

STEP V

Showings, feedback, and offers

I track every showing and every piece of feedback. You get a real update from me weekly, or sooner if something changes. We connect every two weeks specifically about price. The market shifts fast right now and we cannot afford to go stale.

Before we accept any offer, I interview the buyer's lender to make sure we are not setting ourselves up for a financing fall-through three weeks in.

STEP VI

Negotiation, contract, and pre-opening escrow

We negotiate to your goals. Once we accept, we open escrow. I also pre-open escrow on listings whenever possible so we can review the preliminary title report in advance and catch any surprise liens, easements, or title issues before they become buyer objections.

STEP VII

Inspection, appraisal, contingencies

This is where most deals get bumpy and where most of the heavy lifting happens. The buyer schedules inspections, comes back with a list of things they want fixed or credited. I help you decide what is reasonable, what is a stretch, and where to push back. The appraisal can come in low and require a strategy decision. Their loan has to clear underwriting. Title can turn up something nobody saw.

Every one of these has the potential to derail the deal, and every one of them is what you hired me for. My TC keeps every deadline on track and I keep the people on track.

STEP VIII

Final walk-through and close

You sign at title, the buyer funds, the deed records. You hand over the keys. We celebrate.

06 · The 70%
06 What you're hiring me for

Off-market doesn't mean less work.

Distribution is just one part of selling a home. Pricing, negotiation, risk management, and transaction quarterbacking are the other 70% of the job. None of that changes when we go off-market. Here's what your fee actually buys.

Pricing your home correctly (and re-pricing as the market moves)

The single biggest determinant of whether your home sells fast and at top dollar is the price you set on day one. Overpricing kills momentum. Underpricing leaves money on the table. I have seen the cost of getting this wrong, and I will not let it happen to you.

Then we don't set it and forget it. We connect every two weeks specifically about price. The market shifts fast in 2026, and a listing that was priced right four weeks ago can be stale today. Catching it early is the difference between a small adjustment and a desperate one.

Reading buyer feedback and adjusting

Every showing tells us something. Most agents don't collect feedback systematically, and most who do don't know what to do with it. I track every showing, look for patterns, and bring you a recommendation when the data says we need to move.

Negotiating the offer (and the inspection, and the appraisal)

The first negotiation is the offer. Most sellers think this is the only one. It is not. The bigger negotiation is usually after the buyer's inspection, when they come back asking for credits or repairs. I have negotiated hundreds of these. I know what is reasonable, what is a stretch, and when to push back hard.

If you want to remove the inspection unknown entirely, we can do our own pest and home inspection before listing. You either fix the issues or price the home accordingly.

Managing the 100 things that can go wrong

Buyer financing falls through. This is one of the top deal-killers, which is why I interview the buyer's lender before we accept the offer. If the lender is a national call center or a slow institution, I want to know before we are 30 days into escrow.

Beyond financing: appraisal comes in low, title turns up an issue, buyer gets cold feet, inspector flags something scary. Any of these can kill a deal if not handled right. My job is to see the problem early and solve it before it becomes a deal-killer.

Telling you the truth

This is the one most agents fail at. When the price needs to come down, I will tell you. When the offer is bad and you should counter, I will tell you. When you should accept and move on, I will tell you. You hired me for my judgment, not to make you feel good.

07 · Compensation
07 Understanding compensation

Same fee. Different distribution.

After the NAR settlement, every real estate transaction now requires both buyers and sellers to think about agent compensation directly. This used to be invisible to most clients. It isn't anymore. Here's why off-market still earns the same fee.

My listing-side compensation
3%

Covers everything in the distribution section above plus the work the buyer never sees. Investor network outreach, broker network distribution, pricing strategy, negotiation, transaction management, and full compliance from agreement through close. The marketing channel is one variable. The work is the same.

If we transition to full MLS
No change

If off-market doesn't bring the right offer and we transition to a full MLS listing, your fee stays at 3%. Photography, drone, video, social ads, open houses, all of it, no additional charge. The investor work we already did becomes pre-launch interest for the public launch.

Buyer-agent compensation
A separate negotiation

You will decide whether to offer compensation to the buyer's agent and at what level. There is no required amount. I will walk you through what makes strategic sense based on your price point, your buyer pool, and current market dynamics. In most regional price ranges right now, offering 2 to 2.5% to the buyer's side keeps you competitive. We will look at the math together.

Bottom line: I don't hide my numbers. You should know exactly what you are paying and exactly what you are getting for it. That is the same standard I hold any vendor I hire to.

08 · Why an Agent
08 Why you need an agent in 2026

Zillow can't tell you the comp on the next block had cats living in it for a decade. I can.

Every year someone publishes an article saying agents are obsolete. Zillow has photos. AI can write a description. You can find a house online in five minutes. So what are you paying me for?

You're paying for judgment

Anyone can pull up a comp on Redfin. Knowing which comps actually matter for your property, in this market, this month, with these conditions... that is judgment built over thousands of transactions. It is the difference between leaving $30,000 on the table and not.

You're paying for risk management

California disclosures alone run 100+ pages per transaction. The contract is dense. Inspection responses, financing contingencies, appraisal gaps, title issues. Every one of these has trapped a buyer or seller who tried to do it themselves. My job is to see the trap before you walk into it.

You're paying for negotiation that doesn't get personal

When you're negotiating directly with the other side, every word feels personal. When I am negotiating, it is not personal. I can push harder, hold longer, and walk away if I need to, in ways that protect your relationship with the other party and the deal.

You're paying for who I know

Lender, title, escrow, inspector, contractor, painter, mover, stager, photographer, attorney. After 15 years and a network I've spent decades building in this region, I have a vetted referral for every one of these. You inherit that network the moment you hire me.

04 · The Process
04 The buying process

From consultation to handing you the keys.

Most buyer transactions take 30 to 60 days from offer to close, but the full process from our consultation to your keys can take a few months depending on inventory and your timeline. Here's how it goes.

STEP I

Consultation

We talk about what you want, what you can afford, and what's actually available. I ask a lot of questions. I want to understand not just what you're looking for, but why.

STEP II

Lender pre-approval

If you haven't already, you talk to a lender. What matters is getting a real pre-approval letter, not just a pre-qualification.

Who you work with matters as much as the rate. Big banks, credit unions, and online lenders are often slow, hard to reach, and run by underwriting departments that don't pick up the phone. Listing agents know which lenders close on time and which don't, and they advise their sellers accordingly. An offer with a strong local lender behind it gets accepted over an offer with a faceless online lender behind it, even at the same price.

STEP III

Buyer representation agreement

Before I show you a single home, we sign a buyer representation agreement. This is required after the NAR changes. It lays out my 2.5% compensation, the term of our agreement, and exactly what I'm committing to do for you. I'll walk you through every line before you sign.

STEP IV

Search and showings

I set you up on a real-time MLS search filtered to your criteria. You see homes the second they hit. When you find ones you want to tour, we schedule and go together. I do not send you to tour homes alone. I see things you will not, and I want to be there when you're forming your impression.

Two specific exceptions: open houses are fine to drop into on your own (just tell the listing agent up front that you're working with me), and new construction is the one place where touring without me first can cost you. The builder's sales rep is not your agent. Once I've introduced you to a builder formally, you can visit the models with or without me. The introduction is what protects you.

STEP V

Offer and negotiation

When we find the one, I prepare the offer. Strategy starts with price, but it doesn't end there. We talk through contingencies, financing terms, escalation clauses if multiple offers are expected, close timeline, and what we ask the seller to leave behind.

Before we submit, I review the comparable sales one more time and call the listing agent to get a read on what the seller actually cares about. Sometimes it's price. Sometimes it's close timing or a rent-back or a clean offer with no contingency drama. I structure the offer to win, not just to bid.

STEP VI

In contract

Once your offer is accepted, we open escrow. You wire your earnest money to the title company within three days. The clock starts on contingencies, usually 17 days for inspection, 21 for appraisal and loan, depending on what we negotiated.

My TC takes over deadline tracking from here. You'll get a transaction timeline showing every milestone and what's due when. I'm still your point of contact for everything, but you can reach Erika S. directly for paperwork questions.

STEP VII

Inspections and contingencies

We schedule home inspection, pest, and any specialty inspections (septic, well, pool, roof). I review every report with you and we decide what to ask the seller for.

Sometimes the inspection report is not for negotiating right now. Sometimes it's a document you keep for future repairs you plan to do anyway. We focus negotiations on big systems and real safety issues, not every small thing the inspector flagged. Asking for everything kills deals. That said, you're the client, and if you want me to go after something, I will. I'll just walk you through the pitfalls first.

STEP VIII

Appraisal and loan

Your lender orders the appraisal. We work through any underwriting conditions. You stay in close contact with your loan officer.

STEP IX

Final walk-through and close

Within five days before closing, we walk through to make sure the home is in the agreed-on condition and any negotiated repairs were completed. You sign at title, your loan funds, and we record. You get the keys and we celebrate.

05 · The Real Work
05 What you're hiring me for

What a buyer's agent actually does for you.

In a market where you can find every listing online in two seconds, the question is fair: what does a buyer's agent really do for you? Here's the honest list.

I represent only you

The listing agent works for the seller. Their job is to get the seller the highest price and best terms. Without your own agent, you're negotiating against a professional with no one in your corner. With me, you have someone whose only job is to protect your interests. I will not show you a home and quietly hope you buy it because it pays my commission. I will tell you when I think a home is wrong for you, even if it costs me a sale.

I see what you don't

After 15 years of walking through homes in this region, I notice things buyers miss. Foundation cues. Roof age. Drainage issues. Bad additions done without permits. HOA red flags. Layout problems that look fine until you live there. I will not just tour the house with you. I will tell you what I see.

That said, ultimately a home is a personal decision. Regardless of my observations or my opinion, you make the call. My job is to make sure you make it with full information.

I structure the offer to win without overpaying

Offer strategy is more than price. It's contingencies, financing terms, close timeline, what you ask the seller to leave behind. A well-structured offer can beat a higher one. I have written hundreds of offers in this region. I know what the listing agent will respond to and what will get tossed.

I handle the inspection negotiation

After your inspection, you'll get a list of things wrong with the house. Some are real, some are noise, some are things you'll want to handle yourself later anyway. I help you decide what to ask the seller to fix or credit, and I negotiate it. We focus on big systems and safety, not every small flag, because asking for everything is how deals die. This is often where you save (or lose) thousands of dollars.

I quarterback the close

From offer-accepted to keys-in-hand, there are 30+ moving parts and a dozen people involved. I keep everyone moving. My TC tracks every deadline. If something is going to be a problem, I see it early and solve it before it threatens your deal.

I'm here after close

Need a contractor? An electrician? A property tax appeal? A vendor referral for anything home-related? Someone to refer your sister to in three years? I'm still your agent after escrow closes. That's how I've built my business... by being the person you call for everything real estate, forever. We're basically BFFs now.

08 · Compensation
08 Understanding compensation

How 2026 compensation actually works.

After the NAR settlement, every real estate transaction now requires both buyers and sellers to think about agent compensation directly. This used to be invisible to most clients. It isn't anymore.

My buyer-side compensation
2.5%

That's my fee for representing you on the buy side. There are three ways it gets paid, depending on what the seller offers in their listing:

1. The seller offers full buyer-agent compensation (most common). You owe nothing additional.

2. The seller offers partial compensation. You either cover the gap, or we negotiate with the seller to make up the difference (often by adjusting the offer price).

3. The seller offers no buyer-agent compensation. You either pay it directly, or we structure the offer to cover it.

Before you ever look at a home with me, we'll sign a buyer representation agreement that lays out the 2.5% in writing. No surprises at closing.

What the seller decides
A separate negotiation

The seller decides whether to offer compensation to your buyer's agent (me) and at what level. There's no required amount. In most regional price ranges right now, sellers offering 2 to 2.5% to the buyer's side stay competitive. We'll see what each listing offers when we start writing offers.

Bottom line: I don't hide my numbers. You'll know exactly what you're paying and exactly what you're getting for it. That's the same standard I hold any vendor I hire to.

08 Why you need an agent in 2026

Zillow can't tell you the comp on the next block had cats living in it for a decade. I can.

Every year someone publishes an article saying agents are obsolete. Zillow has photos. AI can write a description. You can find a house online in five minutes. So what are you paying me for?

You're paying for judgment

Anyone can pull up a comp on Redfin. Knowing which comps actually matter for your property, in this market, this month, with these conditions... that is judgment built over thousands of transactions. It is the difference between leaving $30,000 on the table and not.

You're paying for risk management

California disclosures alone run 100+ pages per transaction. The contract is dense. Inspection responses, financing contingencies, appraisal gaps, title issues. Every one of these has trapped a buyer or seller who tried to do it themselves. My job is to see the trap before you walk into it.

You're paying for negotiation that doesn't get personal

When you're negotiating directly with the other side, every word feels personal. When I am negotiating, it is not personal. I can push harder, hold longer, and walk away if I need to, in ways that protect your relationship with the other party and the deal.

You're paying for who I know

Lender, title, escrow, inspector, contractor, painter, mover, stager, photographer, attorney. After 15 years and a network I've spent decades building in this region, I have a vetted referral for every one of these. You inherit that network the moment you hire me.

10 · Talk Through
10 A few things we should know

Help me prep before we go.

Now that we're moving forward, there are a few things I need to confirm this week so I can set up your search, intro you to lenders if needed, and start showing you homes. Here's the short list.

Pre-approval status

Have you talked to a lender? If yes, who? If no, that's fine, I'll introduce you to Kristin Cooper at Borrow Smart Home Loans or Lindsay McCoy at CARE Finance Group. Both are excellent. Local lender vs. big bank or online matters more than you'd think — sellers and listing agents pay attention.

Down payment and funds

How much down payment do you have available, and is it liquid (in checking/savings, not in stocks or a 401k that needs to be cashed out)? If you're using gift funds from family, that has its own paperwork. The clearer I am on your funds situation, the better I can structure offers that won't get tripped up in underwriting.

Loan type

Conventional, FHA, VA, USDA? Each has its own quirks — FHA appraisals are stricter, VA has occupancy rules, USDA only works in eligible rural areas. Not a problem either way, just shapes which homes are realistic and how I write your offer.

Current housing

Are you renting (and what's your lease end date)? Selling a current home? Already moved out? Living with family until you find something? This affects timing, contingencies (sale-of-home is a big one), and how aggressive we can be on close dates.

Decision team

Who else is part of this decision? Spouse, partner, parent, financial advisor, anyone else on the loan? I want everyone in the room (or on the call) for the consultation, and I want to know who needs to weigh in before we write any offers.

Timing and what you want

When do you ideally want to be in the home? Driven by lease end, school year, a job change, baby coming, family reunion, just ready? Your timeline plus your must-haves shape the search strategy. The more I know, the less time we waste on homes that won't work.
